Experiment 1 — Marginal Return on SPA Over-Investment (March)
10× more SPA spend returned just ₹0.5L more GMV — proving the demand ceiling
The Demand Ceiling Proof: Jan SPA ₹0.47L → ₹18.2L GMV (38×). March SPA ₹5.17L → ₹16.0L GMV (3×). Spending 11× more yielded less GMV. The extra ₹4.7L generated a marginal ₹−2.2L in GMV — negative incrementality. Efficient advertising captures all available demand. Excess spend does not create demand.
📐 Why SPA Incremental = ~100%
When a shopper searches "multivitamin" or "[brand name]" on a pharmacy platform, they are in active purchase mode. If the brand's ad doesn't appear, a competitor's does — and they buy from them instead. At 38–42× ROAS, virtually every click converts, meaning advertising is not accelerating a purchase that would happen anyway; it is intercepting demand that would leave.
📉 Experiment 2 — April Is the Live Proof
After March's ROAS collapse (3.09×), SPA efficiency in April is 4.68× — still not recovered. Projected April GMV: ₹7.73L vs ₹23.4L average. That ₹15.3L gap is exactly what advertising incrementality looks like when it erodes: competitors have reclaimed search placements and accumulated Quality Score advantage.
🎯 The Optimal Spend Point
Jan–Feb data establishes the efficient operating range: ₹47K/month SPA captures ₹18–20L GMV. Beyond this, returns diminish sharply (March proved it). Below this, share erodes. The brand has a precise ₹47K SPA sweet spot — every rupee above it is wasted, every rupee below it cedes demand to competitors.
